ABOUT THE DATASET

Our dataset is collected from World Happiness Report 2024. The link to the dataset can be found here (Data for Table 2.1). The dataset contains 2,363 rows and 11 columns. Here’s a brief explanation of each column in the dataset:

  • Country name: The name of the country.
  • Year: The year for which the data is reported.
  • Life Ladder (Ladder/Happiness score): The happiness score for each country, based on responses to the Cantril Ladder question that asks respondents to think of a ladder, with the best possible life for them being a 10, and the worst possible life being a 0.
  • Log GDP per capita: The natural logarithm of the country’s GDP per capita, adjusted for purchasing power parity (PPP) to account for differences in the cost of living between countries.
  • Social support: The national average of binary responses (0=no, 1=yes) to the Gallup World Poll (GWP) question “If you were in trouble, do you have relatives or friends you can count on to help you whenever you need them, or not?”
  • Healthy life expectancy at birth: The average number of years a newborn infant is expected to live in good health, based on mortality rates and life expectancy at different ages.
  • Freedom to make life choices: The national average of binary responses to the GWP question “Are you satisfied or dissatisfied with your freedom to choose what you do with your life?”
  • Generosity: The residual of regressing the national average of GWP responses to the donation question “Have you donated money to a charity in the past month?” on log GDP per capita.
  • Perceptions of corruption: The average of binary answers to two GWP questions: “Is corruption widespread throughout the government or not?” and “Is corruption widespread within businesses or not?”
  • Positive affect: The average of previous-day affect measures for laughter, enjoyment, and doing interesting things.
  • Negative affect: The average of previous-day affect measures for worry, sadness, and anger.

DATA CLEANING

After conducting further investigation, we identified some NA values in the dataset. To address this issue, we chose “Mean/Average Imputation” as an appropriate method for handling missing values, specifically for numerical variables. This method replaces missing values with the mean of the available data in that variable.

However, for the Log GDP per capita variable, where GDP grows over time, we applied a different strategy. In this case, we imputed missing values using the average of the two closest available values (one before and one after the missing entry), reflecting the natural progression of GDP over the years.

DATA VISUALIZATION

In this section, we explored the factors that contribute to our happiness by posing specific questions and visualizing the answers through plots.

How happy is our world?

As we can see from the graph, the two regions with the highest scores, Western Europe and North America/ANZ, experience a slight downtrend in recent years. In the first period (2005-2006), East Asia and Latin America both show a dip, which could be linked to economic adjustments and early developmental challenges.

During the 2008-2009 global financial crisis (GFC), Western Europe experiences a drop, while Latin America peaks, possibly due to Latin America’s increased commodity exports during that time, benefiting from global demand.

In 2020-2021, when the COVID-19 pandemic hits, South Asia peaks briefly before dropping sharply, reflecting the region’s initial resilience followed by pandemic-related struggles. Meanwhile, Commonwealth of Independent States and Latin America drop during the pandemic but show an increase afterward as these regions begin to recover. Other regions show relatively stable happiness scores, ranging between 5 and 6, with less volatility during these global events.

How happiness is distributed?

Looking at the boxplot, we can clearly see that the world is not uniformly happy. North America/ANZ and Western Europe have the highest median happiness scores with low variability, indicating a more consistent experience of well-being across these regions. In contrast, Sub-Saharan Africa, South Asia, and the Middle East and North Africa exhibit the lowest median happiness scores, with greater variability, especially in Sub-Saharan Africa, where experiences of happiness are more uneven.

Regions like Latin America, East Asia, the Commonwealth of Independent States, and Southeast Asia fall in the middle range, with median happiness scores between 5 and 6. These regions tend to have larger interquartile ranges compared to Western regions, reflecting more diverse experiences of happiness. Southeast Asia, in particular, shows a left-skewed distribution, meaning that more countries have higher happiness scores, while a few countries pull the lower end downward.

Notably, Latin America and the Caribbean and Central and Eastern Europe have narrower distributions, indicating more consistent happiness levels across the countries within these regions.

Afghanistan in South Asia and Taiwan, Hong Kong in East Asia are notable outliers in their respective regions. Afghanistan, with its long-standing conflicts and economic difficulties, has the lowest score in its region. Taiwan, on the other hand, experiences higher levels of satisfaction than other East Asian areas, likely due to a stable economy and a relatively democratic governance structure.

Happiness and Economic growth: Does being richer mean being happier?

It seems that money might not buy happiness, but it certainly helps! While countries with lower GDP per capita don’t always have low happiness scores, the graph reveals a clear trend: as GDP rises, so do happiness levels. This suggests that wealth plays a positive role in shaping happiness. However, the relationship levels off after a certain point, where additional wealth brings only modest increases in happiness. In other words, once a country reaches a certain level of prosperity, money alone contributes less to overall happiness, indicating that other factors, like social support and quality of life, become more important in determining well-being.

Happiness and Health: How happy are those living the longest?

As we can see, there is a strong positive correlation: countries where people live longer tend to report higher levels of happiness. In other words, people in countries with higher life expectancy are more likely to feel satisfied with their lives. This connection isn’t limited to life expectancy—other health outcomes, such as lower child mortality, also align with higher happiness. This relationship reflects more than just the link between health and happiness. Countries with higher life expectancy often benefit from other favorable conditions, such as higher income levels, better social protections, and stronger healthcare systems.

For example, Western Europe typically exceeds 70 years of life expectancy and happiness scores regularly above 7.5. This reflects a strong overall well-being, supported by stable healthcare systems and robust social safety nets. Meanwhile, Afghanistan has been severely affected by decades of war, leading to a low life expectancy of 55.2 years and a happiness score of 1.44, with limited healthcare access and continuous instability. Palestine and Iraq face similar challenges, where ongoing conflict and inadequate healthcare systems keep both life expectancy and happiness scores low. For instance, Iraq has a life expectancy of 64.5 years and a happiness score of 5.00.

An interesting exception is Israel, which stands out as an anomaly. Despite ongoing regional conflicts, Israel maintains a relatively high life expectancy of 73 years and a happiness score of 6.78, defying the trend seen in neighboring countries.

Happiness and Society: How culture differences affect happiness?

In general, happiness is strongly tied to the level of social support that individuals receive. In our definition, social support refers to the national average of binary responses (0 for No, 1 for Yes) to the question of whether people have relatives or friends they can count on in times of trouble. To further explore the relationship between social support and happiness, let’s take a look at the impact of cultural differences:

  • In collective cultures, individuals see themselves as part of a tightly knit social framework. Social relationships, especially with family and close friends, are central to one’s identity and well-being. There is often a sense of duty and obligation to support each other, which strengthens social bonds and increases the sense of security within the community. Notable countries that are considered collectivist include: China, India, Vietnam, Indonesia, and Mexico.

  • Individualistic cultures, in contrast, prioritize personal independence and autonomy. While social relationships are still important, they are typically more voluntary and less obligatory. Individuals may choose who to rely on in times of need, and social support tends to be more focused on immediate family or close friends rather than larger community or extended family networks. Notable individualistic countries include: Denmark, Sweden, Netherlands, Germany, and France.

In individualistic societies, social support is often voluntary, driven by personal freedom and generosity. While people may rely on friends and family for support, there is generally less societal pressure for such support to be obligatory. This freedom of choice in relationships can contribute to higher levels of perceived happiness when support is provided willingly. In contrast, collectivistic societies often feature more robust and integrated social support systems, but these can also come with stronger expectations for mutual obligation. The cultural responsibility to support others can lead to strong community bonds, but it may also diminish a sense of personal freedom, which can affect individual happiness.

Moreover, cultural norms play a crucial role in how people from different societies report their happiness and social support. Individuals from collectivist cultures may be less likely to express dissatisfaction openly due to a strong cultural emphasis on maintaining harmony and prioritizing the well-being of the community over personal grievances. Conversely, those from individualistic cultures, where self-expression is highly valued, may feel more comfortable sharing honest—even negative—feelings in surveys or questionnaires. This difference in self-reporting provides another layer of complexity in understanding the interplay between culture, social support, and happiness.

Happiness and Freedom: Is free will the key to true fulfillment?

Next, to further validate the affect of culture differences, we will examine how freedom interact with happiness.

The plot further shows that in individualistic cultures, where personal autonomy is central, higher personal freedom often leads to greater happiness. People have more control over their own decisions, allowing them to shape their lives according to their own preferences and goals. This freedom fosters a stronger sense of well-being because individuals can pursue what truly matters to them.

The ability to freely choose relationships and social networks—based on personal desire rather than obligation—also contributes to more meaningful connections, further enhancing happiness. Additionally, having the freedom to pursue personal ambitions, whether in career or lifestyle, gives individuals a strong sense of control and satisfaction in their lives. Ultimately, this sense of independence and self-direction is a key driver of happiness in individualistic societies.

The Happiness equation

Correlation matrix

After examining the individual relationships between Happiness Score and key variables such as Expectancy, Social Support, Freedom, and Log(GDP per Capita), it is evident that each variable contributes to the overall well-being of a country in different ways. However, these variables do not exist in isolation. To better understand the relationships between all the factors, we begin by analyzing the correlation matrix

The correlation matrix confirms the strong positive relationships between Happiness Score and factors like Log GDP per Capita (0.73), Social Support (0.79), and Freedom (0.63). It’s intuitive to expect that wealth, strong social networks, and personal freedom are key elements of what people associate with happiness and a fulfilling life.

It also indicates that Healthy Life Expectancy is influenced by other factors, such as Log GDP (0.82) and Social Support (0.71), in its relationship with Happiness Score (0.74). This suggests that while health is crucial to well-being, it is closely tied to broader economic and social factors.

Interestingly, Generosity shows a more nuanced relationship with happiness, with a lower correlation of 0.05, implying that its impact on happiness score may be more complex and indirect.

CONCLUSION

Our visualizations reveal that happiness is far from a universal concept; instead, it reflects a complex interconnect of economic, social, and cultural factors.

Unsurprisingly, countries with higher GDP per capita, such as those in Western Europe and North America, consistently report higher levels of happiness. These regions benefit from stable healthcare systems and robust social protections, which provide a strong foundation for well-being. In contrast, nations in regions like the Middle East, marked by conflict and limited access to essential services, show significantly lower happiness levels. This stark disparity highlights the crucial role that basic needs and security play in life satisfaction.

Our Pooled OLS regression model, explaining 74% of the variance in happiness scores, underscores the significance of social support, freedom, and healthy life expectancy as key drivers of happiness. Personal autonomy, captured in the measure of freedom to make life choices, emerged as a particularly influential factor. Interestingly, generosity, while relevant, has a more nuanced and indirect influence on happiness.

Cultural context is central to understanding global happiness. In collectivist societies like those in East Asia and South Asia, happiness often stems from strong community bonds and shared responsibilities. Conversely, individualistic cultures in Europe and North America emphasize personal freedom and self-reliance, which also contribute substantially to well-being. These cultural differences underscore that while happiness is a universal aspiration, the pathways to achieving it vary widely based on societal values and norms.

The findings from this study highlight the importance of focusing on strengthening healthcare systems, enhancing social support networks, and promoting personal freedom to improve well-being. Prioritizing these areas, particularly in regions with lower happiness scores, could lead to significant improvements in global life satisfaction.
